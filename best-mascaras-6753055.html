    Things to Consider Before Buying Mascara

    Formula

    When choosing a mascara, note that the formula will affect the results — giving you more length, volume, curl, or all three. Thicker formulas definitely volumize, but can be subject to clumping and smudging if you’re not careful during application. Thinner formulas are better for precision and a more natural look, but might not bring the drama the same way. So if you’re a more experienced mascara user and want big, show-stopping lashes, go for a thick formula. Those who just want a little extra definition without hiding their natural lashes (as well as those trying out mascara for the first time) should consider a thinner formula (hint: the wand is usually more narrow).

    Wand Type

    Speaking of wands — and by wand we mean the applicator/brush — this aspect of mascara also affects the type of lashes you’ll see in the end. Thicker barrel brushes tend to create more volume and length, but also absorb a lot of product, and sometimes cause clumping and smudging if not careful. You’ll find these wands with mascaras like IT Cosmetics Lash Blowout Volumizing Mascara.

    Alternatively, narrow wands may not bring the drama, but they do help with precision, length, and for bottom lashes especially. The third type of wand is an hourglass shape — like the wands found with Too Faced Better Than Sex Mascara. These can help with curling, length, and volume, but can also help you control the stroke a bit better to coat larger and smaller lashes without too much worry of smudging or clumping.

    Shades

    Another thing to take into consideration is the mascara’s shade. You might think all mascaras are black, but some come in several shades of black as well as other colors like brown, blue, pink, and even white. Your application technique will stay the same regardless of what color you choose, but with lighter shades you might have to layer with a few coats of mascara to see the pigment come through.

    If you have lighter hair (like blonde, gray, or white), you might consider brown mascara for a more natural look, as it will still define your lashes but not as intensely as a jet black shade. That being said, “I love black mascara on everyone,” says Medhin.

    Frequently Asked Questions

    • What is tubing mascara and how is it beneficial?

      Tubing mascara is a type of formula that uses polymers to create tiny tubes around each lash rather than painting them like a traditional mascara. These tubes dry instantly and are water-resistant, and they’re not subject to clumping, smudging, running, and other forms of transfer. “Tubing mascara is great for people who tend to get a lot of transfer because of long lashes, eye shape or general watery eyes,” says Medhin.

    • Should you put mascara on bottom lashes?

      Adding mascara to bottom lashes certainly creates more definition. “I love that look,” says Medhin. “I think it really opens up the eye and makes it look bigger. It's also an easy way to define the eye.” It’s easier to use a narrow wand and thinner formula for bottom lashes, but you can use a thick volumizing mascara too, if you’re careful. Tilt your head down when applying the mascara and look up at your mirror to make sure you’re getting each lash. Wiggle the wand the same way you would apply upper lash mascara, moving side to side at the base and then pulling product through to the ends.

    • Which is the best mascara for volume and length?

      “Go for a thicker formula,” says Medhin. While they can be more difficult to apply sometimes, thick mascaras bring the drama when you’re seeing next-level volume and length. When working with a thick formula like COVERGIRL Lash Blast Mascara or Pat McGrath Labs FetishEyes Mascara, make sure to remove any excess product first by wiping the wand sides off inside the tube, then you can maneuver the brush better without risk of clumping or smudges.

      These formulas are highly buildable, which adds to their suitability for volume and length. Start at the base of the lash and wiggle the brush side to side to get the coat started and then pull through up the ends. Repeat this a few times for maximum definition.
